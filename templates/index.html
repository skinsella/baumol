Baumol's cost disease in Ireland

Two competing stories about why Irish service-sector costs keep rising. This site tests both against the public data and reports the verdicts.

The classical Baumol-Bowen account is mechanical: in a high-income economy with integrated labour markets, wages in low-productivity-growth sectors must rise to attract workers, so unit labour costs and relative prices in those sectors drift upward. The competing story — recently advanced in 2026 popular commentary — is institutional: Irish costs are inflated by rent extraction enabled by absent state capacity, with a recurring "failure premium" that has nothing to do with productivity.

These are empirically distinguishable in many places. Where Irish cost growth tracks what Baumol mechanics predict at Ireland's wage level, the institutional story loses purchasing power. Where Irish costs diverge from peers controlling for wages, it gains. This site decomposes the gap.

The price wedge

The most direct visual evidence consistent with a Baumol mechanism is the divergence of services and goods prices in Ireland's HICP. Since 2000 service prices have roughly doubled while goods prices have barely moved. Whether this gap is caused by productivity divergence — Baumol's prediction — or by something else is what the regressions on later pages decompose.
