Baumol's cost disease in Ireland

Why do Irish services keep getting more expensive while goods barely move? This site tests two competing answers against the public data and reports which one the data support.

The two stories, in plain terms

Story 1 — Baumol's cost disease. A barber takes the same time to cut hair today as in 1970, but a factory worker now produces far more cars per hour. Because workers can move between sectors, the barber's wage has to rise to keep up with the factory wage. Result: services get steadily more expensive relative to goods, even though nothing has gone wrong. This is mechanical, not a failure.

Story 2 — the "failure premium". Irish costs are high because the State outsources core functions (housing, asylum reception, healthcare staffing) to private actors who charge a margin. The high prices reflect institutional choice, not productivity arithmetic.

These predict different patterns in the data. Where Irish service-cost growth matches what we'd expect from a high-wage economy with low service-sector productivity growth, Story 1 wins on that question. Where Irish prices are inflated beyond what wages and productivity can explain, Story 2 wins.
