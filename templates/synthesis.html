What do the data say?

This page integrates the evidence from the labour-cost analysis, the productivity regressions, the Department of Finance / ESRI replication and the failure-premium tests into a single answer to the question the site opens with: why do Irish services keep getting more expensive than goods, and what — if anything — should we do about it?

Five numbers that anchor the site.
  • Service prices in Ireland are up about 124% since 2000; goods prices about 23%.
  • The cross-sector spread of pay across Irish sectors stayed near {{ syn.sigma }} (small) while productivity dispersion sat near 0.86 (large) — pay moves together, productivity doesn't.
  • In the EU KLEMS panel, the price-on-productivity coefficient is negative and significant in {{ syn.cross_n_sig }} of {{ syn.cross_n_total }} European countries; Ireland ranks {{ syn.ie_rank }}/{{ syn.cross_n_total }} — middle of the pack on Baumol price-disease.
  • In Eurostat Price Level Indices, Ireland's PLI for state-pricing-heavy categories runs 20–30% above what its overall wage level predicts; for private-market categories it is closer to +8%.
  • Hotels and restaurants HICP rose 29.8% in Ireland 2019→2025 vs 28.8% in the euro area — the excess is 0.9 pp, so the headline price story there is not Irish-specific.

One question, three sources of evidence

Each of the four analytical pages on this site answers a different slice of the same underlying question. Reading them together rather than separately is what allows a clean answer.

The headline answer

Both stories are true, but they describe different things.

The price wedge is mostly Baumol. Service prices have risen much faster than goods prices in Ireland — and in twelve of sixteen comparable European countries. The price-on-productivity regression confirms the mechanism: in sectors that fell behind on productivity, prices rose faster. This is exactly what the 1967 model predicted. The mechanism is not optional and not fixable — it is the mechanical consequence of being a high-wage economy with low productivity growth in person-to-person services.

Specific procurement excesses are the failure premium. Where the State buys services through emergency or constrained procurement — asylum reception in hotels, agency nurses on framework rates — the per-unit cost gap to comparable countries is too large for wage differences to explain. That is institutional and is in principle fixable. It is a real story but a much narrower one than its presentation usually suggests.

What it isn't. A claim that Irish service prices in general reflect a "failure premium" doesn't survive the data. Most of the price divergence is wage-driven Baumol — and most of the wage growth is unexceptional given Ireland's wage level relative to productivity in those sectors.

Where Baumol wins on the evidence

  • The price wedge itself. Services CPI 2024 is around 215 on a 2000=100 basis; goods CPI is around 122. No alternative explanation has been advanced that survives the cross-country test.
  • Compressed pay across sectors. The standard deviation of log hourly labour cost across NACE letters has moved very little since 2008 ({{ syn.sigma }} most recently), while productivity diverged sharply. Pay is moving together while productivity is not — the wage-pull mechanism in Baumol's original model.
  • Price-on-productivity in EU KLEMS. Negative and significant in Ireland (β = {{ syn.our_price_lp }}) and in twelve of sixteen European countries. The pattern is the European norm.

Where the failure-premium thesis wins on the evidence

  • IPAS hotel-bed cost differential. The €99/night-vs-€13.50 Netherlands gap survives any reasonable like-for-like reconciliation. Wage differences between Ireland and the Netherlands are 10-25%, not 500%. Procurement structure is the more parsimonious explanation.
  • Agency-nurse premium. Verified at 1.7–2.0× on basic rates and 2.2–2.8× on premium shifts (HSE framework rates ~€44–70/hr vs permanent fully-loaded ~€25–28/hr). This is real and not Baumol — staff are paid close to permanent scales; the wedge is captured by agencies. Pattern is consistent with publicly-funded health systems facing constrained permanent headcount.
  • Policy mix, not policy failure. The HAP figure (€4bn since 2014, "no units built") is best read not as failure of value-for-money on HAP, but as a critique of where the marginal euro of housing support is being routed — into demand subsidy rather than supply expansion. Whether that is a Baumol-mechanism issue or a policy-choice issue depends on whether you think supply could meaningfully respond at the relevant margin. The construction-cost data say partially yes.

Where the failure-premium thesis loses on the evidence

  • Rent inflation pre-dating HAP. Rents started rising sharply in 2013, before the HAP rollout. Standard event-study identification does not yield a clean "HAP causes rents" coefficient.
  • Hotel and restaurant prices. Of the 29.8% cumulative HICP rise since 2019 in Ireland, only about 41% is wage-explained at the midpoint cost-share assumption (sector-I wages rose 35%; wage cost share is 30–40%). Crucially, the euro area saw a 28.8% rise over the same window — Ireland's excess is just 0.9 percentage points. The non-wage residual (input costs, post-COVID demand recovery, possibly some IPAS supply withdrawal) ran at almost identical rates everywhere in the euro area, so any Ireland-specific institutional channel cannot be the dominant explanation.
  • Cross-country comparison. If high Irish service prices were a uniquely Irish institutional phenomenon, Ireland should look like an outlier. It doesn't. Ireland's price~productivity coefficient ranks {{ syn.ie_rank }} of {{ syn.cross_n_total }} EU KLEMS countries.

What this implies for policy

The two stories require different responses, which is why distinguishing them matters.

If the dominant mechanism is Baumol — as the price evidence suggests — the appropriate policy lever is service-sector productivity. That means investment in healthcare and education productivity (technology, process, training) and tolerance of relative-price drift as the unavoidable cost of being a high-income economy. It does not mean expecting service prices to converge with goods prices. Ageing makes this harder because demand for stagnant-sector services is rising.

If the dominant mechanism in a particular cost line is failure premium — as the IPAS and agency-nurse evidence suggest — the appropriate response is procurement reform and direct state capacity. Note that "more state capacity" and "closing the failure premium" are the same thing for asylum reception (build state-owned reception centres) but opposing things for some healthcare staffing (the constraint there is on permanent headcount expansion within an existing state system).

The shift-share decomposition shows that, so far, sector reallocation toward stagnant sectors has not dragged Irish aggregate productivity growth down. {{ syn.shift_share_summary | safe }} The leading indicator to watch is Hypothesis 6 — when the share of the wagebill in slow-productivity sectors starts rising materially in the data, the aggregate-productivity drag will follow.

What we still don't know

  • Domestic-only Ireland productivity. Headline TFP for Irish C and J sectors is heavily distorted by MNC contract manufacturing and IP onshoring. The CSO Productivity in Ireland release publishes a foreign-vs-domestic split. The next site refresh will run the productivity and replication regressions on the domestic-only series.
  • Causal identification of policy-mix effects on prices. The HAP-vs-rent and IPAS-vs-hotel-prices stories cry out for proper event-study or difference-in-differences identification with local authority or regional variation. Public data are sufficient for this in principle.
  • Reconciling the two wage-on-productivity findings. The EU KLEMS replication finds wage growth uncorrelated with productivity at the sectoral level (the paper's Hypothesis 5, which is what Baumol assumed). Our CSO labour-cost regression finds a negative correlation. The wage measure differs across the two (compensation per employee in EU KLEMS vs hourly labour cost in EHQ03) and that probably explains most of the gap, but not all of it.
