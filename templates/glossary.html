Plain-English glossary

Every term used on this site that might trip up a non-economist reader. Listed alphabetically. The word in bold is what the site uses; the explanation is in plain language.

Baumol's cost disease
The idea that some sectors (services like education, health, hairdressing) get more productive much more slowly than others (manufacturing). Because workers can move between sectors, wages have to rise in the slow sectors to keep up with the fast sectors. The result: prices in slow-productivity sectors rise faster than goods prices, even though nothing has gone wrong. Named for the economist William Baumol, who described it in 1967.
Baumol effect on slope vs intercept
A distinction this site uses to separate two stories. The "slope" is how fast service prices drift up relative to goods prices over time — mechanically driven by Baumol's wage-pull mechanism. The "intercept" is the level prices sit at in any given year — set by market structure, supplier power, and procurement design. Different mechanisms; different data tests.
β (beta), γ (gamma)
Greek letters used as labels for the slopes in the site's regressions. β-convergence asks "do initially low-paid sectors grow faster?". γ asks "do sectors that fell behind in productivity see faster cost growth?" (the headline Baumol prediction). The Greek letters carry no meaning beyond "the slope of this line".
CAGR
Compound annual growth rate. The single annual % growth that, if applied each year, would turn the start value into the end value. Useful when the period is multiple years.
Cobb-Douglas / Tornqvist
Cobb-Douglas is a textbook way to write the production function: output = A × Kα × L1-α, where K is capital and L is labour. Tornqvist is a way of averaging the labour share over two adjacent years to compute a sensible TFP growth rate. Both are standard. The site uses both for constructing TFP growth where EU KLEMS doesn't publish it.
COICOP
Eurostat's classification of consumer expenditure into categories. CP01 is food, CP04 is housing/utilities, CP06 is health, CP11 is restaurants and accommodation, etc. Used in the failure-premium and steelman pages to slice the price level by purpose.
Compensation of employees / hourly labour cost
What a firm actually spends on workers, per hour: wages and salaries plus the employer's social-insurance contributions, training costs, and miscellaneous non-wage labour costs. The CSO publishes this as EHQ03; Eurostat as the labour cost index. The Baumol prediction is about cost, not just wages.
EU KLEMS
A research database covering output, employment, capital and intangibles by sector for European countries from 1995. Built by a consortium of European research institutes; maintained by the Luiss Lab in Rome. Underpins the replication page.
Failure premium
A 2026 essay's term for the extra cost the State pays when it outsources a public function (asylum reception, healthcare staffing, housing) instead of producing it directly. The argument is that absent in-house production capacity, the State enters the market under time pressure and accepts whatever price clears, which produces a premium relative to direct provision.
Fixed effects
A way of controlling for things that don't move within a unit. "Sector fixed effects" absorb the average level of each sector. "Year fixed effects" absorb common-to-all-sectors annual shocks (a recession, a post-pandemic recovery). After fixed effects, the regression coefficient captures only the within-unit relationship — sector-by-year deviations from the sector and year averages.
HAP — Housing Assistance Payment
The Irish State's main rent-subsidy programme, launched in 2014. Pays a fraction of the market rent for low-income households in private rentals. Often invoked in the failure-premium argument. HAP is a demand-side subsidy, not a capital programme, so comparing its cost to social-housing capital outlay is a category mismatch.
HICP — Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices
The Eurostat-defined consumer price index. Used to measure inflation and compare price levels across EU countries on a common basis.
HSE — Health Service Executive
The State agency responsible for providing public health and social services in Ireland. Major buyer of agency-supplied nursing and clinician hours; subject of failure-premium claim 8.
Imputed rent
The rent owner-occupiers would pay themselves if they didn't own their home. Counted in National Accounts as a service produced and consumed by the household, even though no money changes hands. Why "real estate activities" (NACE L) shows artefactually huge productivity per hour and is dropped from the headline regression.
IPAS
The International Protection Accommodation Service, the Irish State's provider of accommodation for asylum-seekers awaiting their hearing. Heavily reliant on contracted hotel beds since 2014, which is the basis of failure-premium claim 3.
MNC distortion
Ireland-specific: large multinationals book activity in Ireland that doesn't reflect domestic production. Contract manufacturing, IP onshoring, aircraft leasing, and corporate domiciliation all inflate sectoral GVA in manufacturing (C) and ICT (J). The site's headline regression excludes these sectors as a robustness check.
NACE
The European industrial classification. Letter-level NACE: A (agriculture), B (mining), C (manufacturing), D (electricity), E (water), F (construction), G (retail), H (transport), I (accommodation/food), J (ICT), K (finance), L (real estate), M (professional services), N (admin services), O (public admin), P (education), Q (health), R (arts), S (other services). The site uses NACE letters throughout.
NTPF — National Treatment Purchase Fund
The State agency that pays private hospitals to clear public waiting lists. Often cited in the failure-premium argument as a multiplier of healthcare cost via outsourcing.
PLI — Price Level Index
Eurostat's cross-country price-level measure. PLI = 100 means the country's prices in that category equal the EU27 average. PLI = 150 means 50% more expensive. Used on the steelman page to compare Irish prices in specific categories with the European panel after PPP adjustment.
PPP — Purchasing Power Parity
An exchange rate that equalises the cost of a representative basket of goods and services across countries. PPPs are the basis for cross- country price-level comparisons (PLIs). PPP-adjusted figures answer "how expensive is this country, in real terms, vs others?".
Productivity, labour vs total factor
Labour productivity (LP) = output per hour worked. TFP (total factor productivity) is what's left when you've accounted for both capital and labour inputs — sometimes called "the residual" or "technology". Both are used in this site; LP is more directly observable.
RTB — Residential Tenancies Board
The Irish State agency that maintains the rent-tenancy register and publishes the rent-index data used in failure-premium claim 6.
σ-divergence
The cross-sector standard deviation of (log) hourly labour cost. A measure of how spread out pay is across sectors. Falling σ means sectors are paying more similar rates over time; rising σ means divergence. Plain-English: "how far apart sectors are paying their workers".
Shift-share decomposition
A way of asking "how much of aggregate productivity growth came from changes in sector mix, vs changes within sectors?". Used to test Baumol's growth-disease prediction (Hypothesis 6 in the replication).
Steelman / strawman
A "steelman" is the strongest version of an argument; a "strawman" is the weakest. The site's steelman page deliberately reformulates the failure-premium thesis in its strongest form (slope vs intercept) and then tests that.
TFP — Total Factor Productivity
See "Productivity" above. Computed as the residual after capital and labour inputs are accounted for. Standard in growth accounting.
ULC — Unit Labour Cost
Hourly labour cost divided by output per hour. The most directly Baumol-relevant single number: it captures how much labour cost is required to produce one unit of output. Mentioned but not the headline focus on this site, since separating labour-cost growth from productivity growth is more informative.
