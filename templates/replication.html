Replication: Department of Finance & ESRI (January 2026)

Hennessy, Lawless & O'Connor (2026) test six of Baumol's diseases on EU KLEMS & INTANProd data, 1995–2021, across 16 EU countries and 16 NACE sectors. This page reproduces their Ireland-only specifications (Tables 1–5, column 4) from the same underlying data and reports cell-by-cell agreement.

Setup

Sample: {{ rep.sectors_used | length }} NACE letter sectors ({{ rep.sectors_used | join(', ') }}), Ireland only, {{ rep.year_start }}–{{ rep.year_end }}, n = {{ rep.n_obs }} sector-year observations. Sample size matches the paper exactly (n = 400). Each regression includes sector fixed effects, year fixed effects, and HC3 robust standard errors.

Construction notes:

  • Real GVA growth: Δlog(VA_Q) — chain-linked volume.
  • Nominal GVA growth: Δlog(VA_CP) — current prices.
  • Price growth: Δlog(VA_PI) — value-added deflator, sector-specific.
  • Hours growth: Δlog(H_EMP).
  • Wage growth: Δlog(COMP / EMPE) — compensation per employee.
  • Labour productivity (LP) growth: Δlog(VA_Q / H_EMP).
  • TFP growth: Cobb-Douglas Tornqvist with w = 2-year average labour share (capped to [0.1, 0.9]). Note: EU KLEMS Release 2023 has TFP missing for Ireland in the growth-accounts module; the paper authors had access to a non-public Irish TFP series. Our TFP construction is therefore a close proxy, not an exact replica, and the TFP-based cells are not expected to match the paper numerically.

Reading the headline finding

The empirical pattern that anchors the DFin-ESRI paper is the price~productivity regression: prices rise faster in sectors where productivity grows more slowly. Both their estimate and ours return a negative coefficient with statistical significance, in line with Baumol's prediction. Where the magnitudes differ, the gap is driven by our self-constructed TFP series rather than by a substantive difference in the underlying mechanism.

The strongest cell-by-cell agreement is the hours~LP regression — our β is essentially identical to the paper's, supporting Hypothesis 4 (employment reallocation) at the sectoral level. The wages~productivity regression is the cell where the paper finds essentially zero correlation (consistent with Baumol's uniform-wage assumption); our replication finds a positive coefficient. Possible reasons for the divergence are listed in the methods page.

How this differs from the original

  • The paper runs five specifications per outcome (full sample, EU6 core, non-EU6, Ireland-only, aggregated) — we replicate column 4 only and add a cross-country sweep.
  • The paper has access to a non-public Irish TFP series; we construct ours via Cobb-Douglas Tornqvist on the public capital and labour series.
  • Our extension list includes the cross-country sweep, the Nordhaus shift-share decomposition and a HICP services-vs-goods chart that ties back to Department of Finance commentary published alongside the paper.
