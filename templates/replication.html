Replication: Department of Finance & ESRI (January 2026)

Hennessy, Lawless & O'Connor (2026) tested six of Baumol's predictions on Europe-wide data, 1995–2021, across 16 countries and 16 sectors. This page reproduces the Ireland-only results from the same data, then adds extensions that put Ireland in cross-country context.

What the original paper does

The paper runs five regressions on a panel of sectors and years. Each regression asks: does this outcome (price, output, hours, wages) move with productivity in the way Baumol predicted?

  • Hypothesis 1 — prices grow faster in slow-productivity sectors (predicted).
  • Hypothesis 2 — real output share is constant across sectors (predicted).
  • Hypothesis 3 — nominal output share of fast-productivity sectors falls (predicted).
  • Hypothesis 4 — labour reallocates from fast to slow sectors (predicted).
  • Hypothesis 5 — wages grow at the same rate everywhere (predicted).
  • Hypothesis 6 — aggregate productivity growth is dragged down (tested separately, see below).

TFP construction note: the public EU KLEMS Release 2023 has Ireland's TFP series missing. The paper authors had access to a non-public version. The LP-based cells reproduce the paper closely; the TFP-based cells reproduce direction but not magnitude.

The headline finding

Both the paper and this replication find a negative and significant coefficient in the price-on-productivity regression. Sectors that fell behind in productivity growth saw faster price growth — exactly the Baumol price-disease prediction. This is the most robust result in the paper and survives our re-run.

Extension 1 — fixed-base-year shift-share (Hypothesis 6)

{{ fig(shift_share_chart, 'figure 2', 'Counterfactual aggregate productivity growth — what if sector shares had stayed where they were in year X?', 'Each blue bar is what aggregate Irish labour productivity growth would have averaged if sector weights had been frozen at the year on the x-axis. The red dashed horizontal line is what actually happened.', shift_share_commentary) }}

Baumol's strong-form prediction is that the bars should slope downward to the right — the more recent the base year, the lower the counterfactual growth, because by then the slow sectors had already gained weight. If the bars are flat or trend upward, sector shares haven't moved enough to drag aggregate growth down (which is what the paper found across Europe).

Extension 2 — Ireland in cross-country context

{{ fig(cross_country_chart, 'figure 3', 'Price-on-productivity coefficient, country by country', 'One bar per EU KLEMS country. The bar is the country''s own price~LP coefficient. The black whisker is the 95% confidence interval. Ireland is highlighted in red.', cross_country_commentary) }}

A negative bar means that country shows a Baumol price-disease pattern. Most European countries do — the mechanism is general, not Irish-specific. This matters for the failure-premium argument on the next page: if 12 of 16 European countries see the same wage-pressure-on-services pattern, that pattern in Irish data is hard to attribute primarily to institutional choices specific to Ireland.

Differences from the original

  • The paper runs five different country specifications (full sample, EU6 core, non-core, Ireland-only, aggregated). We replicate column 4 only and add a cross-country sweep that does the headline regression country-by-country instead.
  • The paper has access to a non-public Irish TFP series; we construct ours from the public capital and labour series via Cobb-Douglas Tornqvist.
  • Our extension list adds the cross-country sweep, the Nordhaus shift-share decomposition, and the HICP services-vs-goods chart on the front page that ties back to the Department of Finance Box 5 commentary published alongside this paper.
