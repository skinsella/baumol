Productivity and pay — does the data support Baumol?

Baumol's idea is simple: if some sectors get more productive faster than others but everyone's wages move together, then the slow-productivity sectors get more expensive over time. This page tests that prediction three ways using Irish data.

Test 1 — does pay across sectors stay close together?

{{ fig(sigma_chart, 'figure 1', 'Spread of hourly labour cost across sectors, over time', 'A single line. Higher means sectors are paying very different hourly rates from each other; lower means they have converged. (Technically: standard deviation of log hourly labour cost across NACE letters each quarter.)', sigma_commentary) }}

Baumol predicts the line stays flat or falls — wages in slow sectors are pulled up to keep the spread modest. A rising line would push back on the mechanism.

Test 4 — does the stagnant-sector wagebill share rise over time?

{{ fig(share_chart, 'figure 4', 'Share of total wagebill: stagnant sectors vs progressive sectors', 'Two lines. The red line is the share of all paid compensation going to traditionally low-productivity-growth sectors (transport, accommodation, education, health, arts, other services). Baumol's third prediction — sometimes called Baumol's disease in the strict sense — is that an ageing, services-using population shifts more of the wagebill into stagnant sectors over time, even if hours don't shift.

What this page does not do (yet)

A full unit-labour-cost decomposition uses sector-specific output deflators, which are heavily distorted in Irish manufacturing (C) and ICT (J) by multinational accounting. The replication page applies the standard EU KLEMS treatment to that problem. The next refresh will add a domestic-only Irish productivity series using the CSO foreign-vs-domestic split.
