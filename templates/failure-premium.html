Testing the "failure premium" thesis

A 2026 essay argued that Ireland's high prices come not from productivity arithmetic but from institutional choice — the State outsourcing things it should provide directly, and paying a margin for the privilege. Each numbered claim from that essay is tested below against public data.

The five possible verdicts, in plain terms

  • REFUTED the data say the claim is wrong.
  • PARTIAL part of the claim holds, part doesn't.
  • CONFIRMED the data support the claim.
  • REFRAMED the numbers are right but the mechanism implied by the claim is misstated. (Typical case: a demand subsidy is compared to capital spending — apples to oranges.)
  • UNTESTABLE can't be checked from public data within this build.
Adjudication standard. Where the institutional thesis survives a test, it is reported as confirmed. The page is intentionally dispassionate — the site does not have a stake in either thesis "winning", only in matching the data.

What the verdicts add up to

No story wins outright. Two patterns stand out:

  1. Where the claim hinges on prices alone (rents up 89%, hotels 27% above 2019, healthcare more expensive than in 2014): the headline number is usually right, but a large share of the rise traces to wage growth in those sectors — which is what Baumol predicts in any high-wage economy. The institutional thesis does not need to be invoked to explain the price level.
  2. Where the claim hinges on procurement structure (asylum hotel-bed cost vs Netherlands, agency-nurse premia): the gap is too large for wages alone to explain. The institutional thesis survives these tests. They are about how the State buys things, not about productivity.

In short: the failure-premium argument is strong on procurement and weak on prices.
