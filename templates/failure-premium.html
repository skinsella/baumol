Testing the "failure premium" thesis

A 2026 essay argued that Ireland's high prices come not from productivity arithmetic but from institutional choice — the State outsourcing things it should provide directly, and paying a margin for the privilege. Each numbered claim from that essay is tested below against public data.

The five possible verdicts, in plain terms

  • REFUTED the data say the claim is wrong.
  • PARTIAL part of the claim holds, part doesn't.
  • CONFIRMED the data support the claim.
  • REFRAMED the numbers are right but the mechanism implied by the claim is misstated. (Typical case: a demand subsidy is compared to capital spending — apples to oranges.)
  • UNTESTABLE can't be checked from public data within this build.
Adjudication standard. Where the institutional thesis survives a test, it is reported as confirmed. The page is intentionally dispassionate — the site does not have a stake in either thesis "winning", only in matching the data.

What the verdicts add up to

No story wins outright. Three patterns stand out:

  1. Headline price claims are mostly correct on level. Rents up 89% (verified +91% via HICP CP041), hotels +27% above 2019 (verified +29.8%) — the underlying numbers hold up.
  2. But those price rises are not Irish-specific. Hotels and restaurants HICP rose 28.8% over the same period in the euro area — Ireland's excess is just 0.9 pp. Most of the rise is non-wage input cost passthrough common to EA20, with a wage-passthrough component that is only about 41% of the total at typical wage shares. Neither the Baumol-wage-pull mechanism nor the institutional failure-premium thesis is the dominant driver of these consumer-facing price rises.
  3. Where the claim hinges on procurement structure, the institutional thesis survives. Asylum hotel-bed cost vs Netherlands (5–7× gap) and agency-nurse premia (1.7× to 2.8× ratio verified) cannot be explained by wage differences. These are about how the State buys things, not about productivity, and they are real.

In short: the failure-premium argument is strong on procurement and weak when applied to broad consumer-price levels — those levels are mostly explained by Europe-wide input-cost dynamics that aren't specifically about Irish institutions.
