Testing the "failure premium" thesis

A 2025 essay argued that high Irish costs reflect a structural "failure premium" — the price of state outsourcing rather than provision. Each numbered claim is tested below against public data. Verdicts are recorded as refuted, partially refuted, confirmed, reframed, or untestable.

Adjudication rule. A claim is refuted only when public data materially contradicts it. A claim is reframed when the underlying number is correct but the inferred mechanism is misspecified (most often: a demand subsidy compared to a capital programme, or a like-for-unlike international comparison). This page is intentionally dispassionate: where the institutional thesis survives a test, it is reported as such.

How to read these verdicts collectively

The pattern across claims matters more than any single one. If most testable claims involving market services (housing rents, hotel prices, healthcare wages) trace to underlying labour-cost dynamics that are unexceptional given Irish wage levels, the failure-premium thesis is more limited than its presentation suggests. If a meaningful subset of claims survives — for instance the asylum hotel-bed cost decomposition, where the international gap is too large for wage differentials alone to explain — that subset deserves the policy weight. The verdicts page is intended as a worked example of where each story applies, not a debating point.
