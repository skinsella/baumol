The steelman case for the failure premium — and the evidence

The institutional thesis that Ireland's high prices reflect a "failure premium" looks weak when tested against generalised consumer-price growth. But there is a sharper version of the same argument that is harder to dismiss. This page sets out the steelman thesis and tests it against public Eurostat data.

Why the original framing fails — and the sharper version that doesn't

The failure-premium argument as usually stated says: Irish service prices are high because the State has chosen to outsource what it should provide directly, and that outsourcing produces rent extraction by private suppliers. Tested against generalised price growth, that claim mostly fails — service prices have risen at roughly the same rate across most European countries, the price-on-productivity coefficient is European-typical in Ireland, and most of the within-Ireland price growth in services traces to wage growth that is unexceptional given Irish wage levels. The slope is Baumol.

The steelman version is structurally different. It says:

Steelman thesis. Baumol explains the slope. The failure premium explains the intercept. Service prices in every advanced economy drift up faster than goods prices — that is mechanical. But the level at which Irish service prices sit, for any given national wage level, is institutionally determined. Where the State is the dominant buyer of a service and lacks in-house production capacity, Irish unit costs run materially higher than peer-country benchmarks even after wage adjustment. That gap is the failure premium, and it is real.

This is a sharper claim because it is consistent with most of the data the rest of the site shows. It does not need to refute the European-typical price-on-productivity coefficient. It locates the institutional damage specifically in the level, which the cross-country regressions on the replication page absorb into country fixed effects by construction.

How to test it

Eurostat publishes Price Level Indices (PLIs) for individual COICOP categories. A PLI of 100 means the country's prices in that category equal the EU27 average; 150 means 50% more expensive. Crucially, PLIs are PPP-adjusted level measures — they let us ask, of two countries, "even though one has higher wages, are its prices in this category higher than that wage gap explains?".

Three steps:

  1. For each EU country, take its PLI for the overall economy (GDP PLI). This proxies the country's general wage and price level.
  2. For each detailed category — health, hospital services, housing, education, transport, hotels, etc. — regress the country's PLI for that category on the country's GDP PLI across the European panel. The fitted relationship tells you what each category's PLI should be at each wage level.
  3. The residual — actual PLI minus predicted — is the country's institutional level premium for that category. If Ireland's residual is large in heavily State-procurement-dependent categories and ~0 in private-market categories, the steelman has empirical support.

Where the steelman doesn't survive

Education is the cleanest negative result. Ireland's education PLI is 20% below what the regression predicts for its wage level. That is consistent with the steelman's central claim: education in Ireland is more directly state-provided than health (free primary, secondary, public-funded university), so the procurement-induced level premium does not arise. The pattern across categories — high excess where the State procures, low excess where the State directly provides — is precisely what the steelman predicts.

Restaurants and hotels show only a 6% excess. The IPAS hotel-bed contracting argument therefore only weakly survives this test at the national level. That is consistent with the failure-premium page's earlier finding: most of the hotel-price rise is wage-driven Baumol; the institutional channel exists but is small relative to the wage component in headline data. Local-area data would be needed to test whether the effect is concentrated in specific markets.

So is the steelman supported by the evidence?

Yes, with caveats.

Supporting. The differential between heavy-State-buyer categories ({{ syn.high_mean }}% mean excess PLI) and low-State-buyer categories ({{ syn.low_mean }}% mean excess PLI) is {{ syn.differential }} percentage points. That gap is the empirical content of the steelman. It survives the test that the original failure-premium argument did not, because it is asking a different question — about levels rather than slopes.

Caveats.

  • The State-buyer-intensity classification is a working categorisation, not a measured value. A formal test would use the actual share of sector demand financed by the State (CSO supply-and-use tables would suffice).
  • Eurostat PLIs for health and hospital services capture the out-of-pocket and public-procurement components recorded in HICP, not the full unit cost of care. A complementary test using OECD health-spending-per-capita PPP data (which captures the full state-funded cost) would tighten the case.
  • The country fixed effects in the price-on-productivity regression on the replication page also absorb some non-institutional variation (currency-area effects, energy import dependence, geography). The PLI test inherits this and the country fixed effect is therefore an upper bound on the failure-premium component.
  • Outsourcing is endogenous. Countries that have weaker in-house state capacity may also be countries where private-market service costs are high for unrelated reasons. A causal test would need a within-country policy switch — the rare cases where Ireland directly provided rather than procured a service for a defined period.

What this changes about the bottom line

The synthesis page's headline conclusion stands but is now more nuanced. The price wedge between services and goods is mostly a Baumol mechanism, visible across Europe and not specifically Irish. But within that European-typical pattern, Ireland sits at the top of the distribution for the level of state-procured services — by 25 to 30% — and that gap is the empirical content of the failure-premium argument. The institutional critique of Irish public procurement is empirically supported, narrower than its original framing suggests, and important.

The policy implication compounds: productivity policy can attenuate the slope (slow the drift) and procurement reform with direct state capacity can attenuate the intercept (lower the level). They are complementary, not substitutes. The numbers say both levers matter and that the second has been under-used in Ireland.
